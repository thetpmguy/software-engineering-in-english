# ✅ Quiz 19 — Your Path Into Tech

Answer in your head or on paper, then open the fold to check. No grades, no timer —
this is for you.

---

**1. True or false: you need a computer science degree to work as a software engineer.**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**False.** Many working engineers have no CS degree — they're self-taught,
bootcamp-trained, or degreed in something unrelated. Employers ultimately screen for
whether you can do the work, and a portfolio of real projects is believable proof
without a diploma.
</details>

---

**2. Also true or false: "you can go from zero to hired in 3 months." What's the honest
timeline for most part-time learners?**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Also **false** — that's the other lie. For most people studying part-time (roughly 8–15
hours a week) around a job or family, zero to genuinely job-ready takes **12 to 24
months** of consistent effort. Long, but walkable — and free of expensive gates.
</details>

---

**3. A 45-year-old nurse asks if she's "too old to start." What does the lesson say —
and what advantage does she have that a 22-year-old graduate doesn't?**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Age is genuinely not the barrier people fear — career-changers in their 30s, 40s, and
50s enter tech constantly. Her advantage is **domain knowledge**: a nurse who codes
understands hospital software better than any 22-year-old ever will. Companies build
software *about* the world, so they need people who know the world. Her first career is
an asset, not dead weight.
</details>

---

**4. Compare the four routes into tech in one honest line each.**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**CS degree:** deepest foundations, slowest and most expensive; opens research and some
big-company doors most readily. **Bootcamp:** fast and structured, costs real money, and
quality varies wildly — interrogate one before paying. **Self-taught:** free or cheap
and fits around real life, but demands discipline and a community. **Apprenticeships /
returnships:** rare but real — you learn while being *paid*, and people forget to search
for them.
</details>

---

**5. Name three questions you should ask a bootcamp before handing over money.**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Any three of: What percentage of graduates work *in the field* within 6–12 months, and
how do you define "employed"? Can I speak to three recent graduates you didn't
hand-pick? Who actually teaches — working engineers or recent graduates of this same
bootcamp? What happens if I fall behind, and what's the refund policy in writing? What
are the exact conditions on the "job guarantee"?
</details>

---

**6. Your friend loves helping people, is endlessly patient, and hates the idea of
coding all day. Name two tech roles that might suit her, and why "getting into tech"
doesn't require becoming a coder.**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Support engineering** (helping stuck users — patience and people-reading are the job)
and **technical writing** (making complicated things make sense — the teacher's superpower). Tech
companies are full of vital roles that write little or no code: QA, project
coordination, data analysis, product, design, developer marketing. For many
career-changers these doors are closer *and* better-fitting — and the building is
connected on the inside if she later wants to move.
</details>

---

**7. Why does the lesson say "a portfolio beats certificates"? Use the baker analogy.**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Certificates prove you *attended*; projects prove you can *build*. Hiring a junior
baker, you'd rather taste three things they baked than read a pastry-school diploma.
Three real, small, **finished** projects on your GitHub — with commit history and honest
READMEs — beat ten certificates, because the hiring manager can taste them directly.
</details>

---

**8. List the five standard stages of a tech interview process, in order.**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Recruiter screen** (does your story make sense?) → **technical screen** (first look at
your technical thinking) → **take-home or live exercise** (how you actually work,
including when stuck) → **onsite/panel** (depth, collaboration, behavioral stories) →
**offer** (negotiable — check levels.fyi first).
</details>

---

**9. What are whiteboard/LeetCode interviews, and what's the lesson's honest take?**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Live algorithm-puzzle interviews used by some companies (especially large ones),
commonly practiced on a site called LeetCode. Honestly: they resemble daily engineering
work about as much as spelling bees resemble writing novels, many engineers dislike
them, and many companies skip them — but they exist, and they're passable with
unglamorous preparation: a few dozen practiced problems over a couple of months, after
your fundamentals are solid, never instead of them.
</details>

---

**10. Recite the learning loop — and explain why "getting stuck" isn't a sign of
failure.**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

**Build something slightly too hard → get stuck → search or ask → finish anyway →
repeat.** Getting stuck isn't failure — it's the mechanism, like muscles that only grow
when the weight is slightly too heavy. Each pass through the loop, "slightly too hard"
moves up a notch. Every self-taught developer learned exactly this way.
</details>

---

**11. What's the community etiquette, in three lines? And why does the lesson insist on
finding a community at all?**

<details><summary>Show answer</summary>

Etiquette: **search before asking** (your question is usually answered somewhere);
**show what you tried** and the exact error when you do ask; **thank people and report
back** what worked. Why a community matters: loneliness — not difficulty — is the main
reason self-taught journeys stall.
</details>

**12. 🗣️ Explain it to a friend**

A friend texts you: *"I'm 38, I manage a restaurant, and I'm miserable. I saw an ad
saying I could become a coder in 12 weeks for $15,000. Should I do it?"*

In under a minute, jargon-free: give them the honest picture — the timeline, the
bootcamp caution, the free alternatives, and one reason their restaurant experience
actually helps.

<details><summary>One possible answer</summary>

"Maybe tech, yes — but slow down on that ad. Nobody reliably goes from zero to hired in
12 weeks; realistically it's one to two years of steady part-time learning, and anyone
guaranteeing faster is selling something. Before paying $15,000, grill them: what
percentage of graduates actually work in the field within a year, and can you talk to
graduates they *didn't* pick for you? Meanwhile, the best starting materials are
genuinely free — freeCodeCamp, The Odin Project, Harvard's CS50 — so you can test
whether you even *like* this for zero dollars before betting fifteen thousand.

And here's the part the ad won't tell you: running a restaurant is tech-relevant
experience. You coordinate chaos on deadlines, manage difficult humans, and keep twenty
moving parts from colliding — that's project management, and tech teams pay for it.
You're not starting from zero. You're transferring."
</details>
